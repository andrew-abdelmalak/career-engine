# LinkedIn Profile Rules

**Confidence levels:** what follows is split into two tiers. Platform Mechanics are verifiable product constraints, treat as reliable. Best-Practice Heuristics are consistent industry consensus from secondary sources, not primary-sourced — treat as directional, not binding. Unsourced marketing stats are deliberately excluded — claims that circulate without a traceable primary source aren't trustworthy enough to act on.

---

## Platform Mechanics (verified constraints)

- **Headline:** 220-character limit. Only the first ~60–70 characters reliably show on mobile search/results — front-load role, specialization, and top keyword there.
- **About section:** 2,600-character limit. Only the first 2–4 lines (~200–300 characters) show before "see more" — open with what you do and for whom, don't bury it.
- **Profile photo:** required for a complete profile; treat as non-optional.
- **Messaging:** direct messages are gated behind a mutual connection (absent InMail/Premium) — the Contact Info section is the one channel a recruiter or non-connection can use to reach the person without connecting first, so it's worth keeping filled in.
- **Connection-request volume:** LinkedIn throttles high-volume connecting algorithmically rather than publishing a fixed cap — sending a large batch in a short window risks a temporary restriction. No specific number is stable enough to state as a rule here; the safe default is a modest, steady pace rather than a burst.

## Best-Practice Heuristics (directional, not hard rules)

- Structure About as: what you do + who for (opening) → 3–5 concrete highlights, quantified honestly wherever the knowledge base supports it, described by scope or method where it doesn't → closing/call-to-action.
- About has room for 2,600 characters but reads stronger around 1,800–2,200 (~300–350 words) — full length isn't the target, focus and scannability are.
- Match Skills entries to the target role's stated requirements, reordered per job, not left in a fixed default order.
- LinkedIn's stated search direction is semantic relevance over raw keyword density, not exact matching — write natural language that happens to include the target terms; 1–2 mentions of a given keyword is enough, don't keyword-stuff.
- A couple of genuine recommendations (e.g. one supervisor, one peer/teammate) reads stronger than zero — flag it as a suggestion if the knowledge base contains a usable quote, don't fabricate one.
- Structure the headline as target role/aspiration + top skills or differentiators + who it's for, not just a job title. This matters most for a thin or early-career profile: a literal label like "Student" or "Fresh Graduate" isn't a term recruiters search for — an aspirational professional title in the actual field (e.g. "Aspiring Structural Engineer" over "Engineering Student") searches better and reads as more deliberate.
- Profile photo: a square, head-and-shoulders crop with the face clearly visible, attire matching the target industry's norm, a real photo over an avatar or AI-generated image. If a personal photo genuinely isn't an option, a simple named graphic is a better fallback than leaving the slot blank.
- Banner image should reflect the person's professional focus, not another selfie — and keep in mind the profile photo overlaps part of the banner's left edge in LinkedIn's layout, so don't put content that needs to stay visible behind it.
- When the Experience section is thin (student, early-career, career switcher), lean on the sections that compensate: relevant coursework or class projects, volunteer work, external certifications or courses outside a degree program, and — where the relationship genuinely supports it — a recommendation from a professor or mentor.

## Outreach & Engagement Etiquette

- Connection requests: always include a short, specific note — what genuinely prompted the request, not a generic line. A blank request is easy to ignore; a specific one isn't.
- Messages to a new or lightly-known connection: open with an actual greeting and a genuine, specific reason for reaching out before making the ask. Leading with the ask reads as transactional.
- Comments on other people's posts: add a real point of view or a specific reaction to the content, not a low-effort "nice post" / "congrats" one-liner — engagement is reciprocal, and substantive comments are what make someone check the commenter's own profile.

## Content Strategy

- A directional starting mix for original posts: roughly 70% educational/expertise content (teach something from the person's own field or experience — this is the highest-value, most-cited share in general content-marketing guidance), 20% authentic personal narrative (a real challenge or lesson, not just a highlight-reel win), 10% timely/trending commentary. Treat as a starting ratio to adjust from, not a quota to hit exactly.
- Personal or lifestyle content only belongs in a post when it's explicitly tied back to a professional or career takeaway — without that framing it reads as off-platform for LinkedIn's audience.
- Consistency over frequency: a modest, sustainable posting cadence maintained over time reads better than a burst of activity followed by silence.
- When repurposing someone else's material — a talk, an interview, a podcast — credit the source explicitly rather than presenting it as original.

---

## Content Rules

- First-person narrative is acceptable in the About section — LinkedIn is a first-person platform, unlike the CV.
- Use bullet symbol • for description line breaks within an entry, not markdown bullets.
- Attach media (certificates, reports, photos) with meaningful titles; leave descriptions blank unless they'd add context not already present in the entry text.
- Never present someone else's writing verbatim as an original post — it undermines credibility if traced back, and LinkedIn's own audience frequently does trace things back.

## Generation Protocol

Living, low-stakes, cheap to re-edit — single draft, then refine directly on feedback. No multi-stage gate.

Deliver as a plain text or markdown code block, not a formal document — this is copy-paste material for LinkedIn's own editor, not a file to generate.
